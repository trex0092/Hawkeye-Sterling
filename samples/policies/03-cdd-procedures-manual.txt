This manual sets out the customer due diligence (CDD) procedures that
every director, officer, employee, contractor and agent of
[Reporting Entity] must follow when establishing or maintaining a
business relationship or carrying out an occasional transaction.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. PURPOSE AND SCOPE
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The purpose of this manual is to provide clear, step-by-step procedures
for identifying and verifying customers, understanding the nature of
their business, assessing the risks they present and keeping that
information current throughout the life of the relationship.

These procedures apply to every customer of [Reporting Entity], whether
the customer is a natural person, a legal person, a legal arrangement
or any other form of entity. They apply equally to one-off transactions
and to continuing business relationships. They apply regardless of
the value of the transaction unless a specific threshold is stated in
this manual.

No business relationship may be established and no transaction may be
executed until the applicable CDD requirements described in this manual
have been completed to the satisfaction of the MLRO or, in the case of
standard CDD, to the satisfaction of the front-office staff member and
the compliance officer assigned to the file.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. REGULATORY FRAMEWORK
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

These procedures are grounded in the following regulatory instruments.

Federal Decree-Law No. 10 of 2025 on Anti-Money Laundering and
Countering the Financing of Terrorism and Financing of Illegal
Organisations. This is the primary AML/CFT statute in force in the
United Arab Emirates. It sets out the obligation to conduct CDD, the
circumstances in which simplified or enhanced measures apply, and the
penalties for non-compliance.

Cabinet Decision No. 10 of 2019 Concerning the Implementing Regulation
of Federal Decree-Law No. 20 of 2018 (as may be amended or replaced
under the new Decree-Law), which provides detailed requirements on
identification, verification, beneficial ownership, record-keeping and
related matters.

FATF Recommendation 22, which requires countries to ensure that dealers
in precious metals and stones are subject to CDD requirements when they
engage in cash transactions at or above the applicable threshold, and
which encourages the application of CDD to all transactions as a matter
of good practice.

Guidance issued from time to time by the Ministry of Economy as the
supervisory authority for DNFBPs in the UAE.

Where a conflict exists between these instruments, the firm will apply
the stricter requirement.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. STANDARD CDD REQUIREMENTS
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Standard CDD is the baseline that applies to every customer unless the
risk assessment indicates that simplified or enhanced measures are
warranted.

Natural persons.

For a customer who is a natural person, the firm will collect and verify
the following information before establishing a business relationship or
executing a transaction.

Full legal name as it appears on the identity document. Date and place
of birth. Nationality and, where different, country of residence.
Current residential address. Occupation or nature of business activity.
A clear copy of a valid, government-issued photo identification document
such as a passport, national identity card or Emirates ID. A second
form of identification or address verification where the risk profile
warrants it.

Verification will be carried out against the original document or a
certified copy. Where the customer is not physically present, the firm
will use reliable electronic verification methods and will apply the
additional safeguards described in the enhanced due diligence section
of this manual.

Legal persons.

For a customer that is a legal person, the firm will collect and verify
the following information.

Full legal name and any trading names. Legal form and proof of
existence, such as a certificate of incorporation, a trade licence or
equivalent registration document. Registered office address and, where
different, the principal place of business. The names of the directors,
partners or equivalent officers. The powers that regulate and bind the
legal person, as set out in the articles of association, partnership
agreement or equivalent governing document. The names and identification
details of the individuals authorised to act on behalf of the legal
person in the proposed business relationship.

The firm will obtain a copy of the constitutional documents and will
verify the registration details against the relevant commercial
register or licensing authority.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. SIMPLIFIED DUE DILIGENCE
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Simplified due diligence (SDD) may be applied only where the firm's
risk assessment has established that the customer, the product, the
delivery channel and the geography all present a demonstrably lower
risk of money laundering or terrorist financing. SDD does not mean no
due diligence. It means that the extent, timing or frequency of certain
CDD measures may be reduced.

The MLRO must approve the application of SDD to any customer category
before it takes effect. The approval must be documented and must state
the specific CDD measures that have been reduced and the rationale for
the reduction.

Examples of situations where SDD may be considered include transactions
with regulated financial institutions in jurisdictions with effective
AML/CFT regimes, and transactions with government entities in the UAE.

Even where SDD is applied, the firm will still collect sufficient
information to identify the customer and will still monitor the
relationship for unusual or suspicious activity. If at any point the
risk profile changes, the firm will upgrade the customer to standard
CDD or enhanced due diligence as appropriate.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. ENHANCED DUE DILIGENCE
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Enhanced due diligence (EDD) applies whenever the firm's risk
assessment identifies a higher risk of money laundering or terrorist
financing. EDD is mandatory in the situations described below. It may
also be applied to any other situation at the discretion of the MLRO.

Triggers for mandatory EDD.

Customers who are politically exposed persons (PEPs) or family members
or close associates of PEPs. Customers from, or transactions involving,
jurisdictions identified by the FATF as high-risk or under increased
monitoring. Non-face-to-face business relationships where the customer
has not been physically present for identification. Complex or unusually
large transactions, or unusual patterns of transactions, that have no
apparent economic or lawful purpose. Correspondent relationships. Any
other situation where the firm has reason to believe that the risk of
money laundering or terrorist financing is elevated.

Politically exposed persons.

When a customer, a beneficial owner or a person authorised to act on
behalf of the customer is identified as a PEP, the firm will take the
following additional steps. Obtain senior management approval before
establishing or continuing the business relationship. Establish the
source of wealth and the source of funds. Conduct enhanced ongoing
monitoring of the business relationship for the duration of the PEP
status and for a reasonable period after the person ceases to hold the
qualifying public function.

PEP status will be determined through screening against commercially
available PEP databases and through direct enquiry of the customer
where the screening results are inconclusive.

High-risk jurisdictions.

When a customer is resident in, incorporated in or conducts significant
business through a jurisdiction that appears on the FATF list of high-
risk jurisdictions subject to a call for action or on the FATF list of
jurisdictions under increased monitoring, the firm will apply
countermeasures proportionate to the risk. These may include collecting
additional information about the customer, the nature of the business
relationship and the intended purpose of the transactions, increasing
the frequency of ongoing monitoring, and requiring the first payment to
be made through an account in the customer's name at a regulated bank.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. BENEFICIAL OWNERSHIP IDENTIFICATION
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

For every customer that is a legal person or a legal arrangement, the
firm will identify and take reasonable measures to verify the identity
of the beneficial owner or owners.

A beneficial owner is any natural person who ultimately owns or controls
the customer, whether through direct or indirect ownership or control,
or any natural person on whose behalf a transaction is being conducted.
Ownership of twenty-five per cent or more of the shares, voting rights
or capital of a legal person is treated as an indicator of beneficial
ownership, but the firm will look beyond formal ownership structures to
identify the natural person who exercises actual control.

If, after taking all reasonable measures, the firm is unable to identify
a beneficial owner through ownership or control, it will identify the
senior managing official of the legal person and document the steps
taken and the reasons why no beneficial owner could be identified.

The firm will not rely solely on the customer's self-declaration of
beneficial ownership. It will verify the information using independent
sources such as commercial registers, annual returns, shareholder
registers and, where available, central beneficial ownership registers.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. SOURCE OF FUNDS AND SOURCE OF WEALTH
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The firm will establish the source of funds for every transaction and
the source of wealth for the business relationship.

Source of funds refers to the origin of the specific funds used in the
transaction. The firm will collect documentary evidence such as bank
statements, sale contracts, loan agreements, payroll records or other
documents that demonstrate where the funds came from.

Source of wealth refers to the origin of the customer's overall
financial standing. It encompasses the activities that have generated
the customer's net worth over time. The firm will collect information
about the customer's occupation, business activities, investments,
inheritances and any other material sources of wealth. The depth of
enquiry will be proportionate to the risk profile of the customer.

Where the customer is unable or unwilling to provide satisfactory
evidence of source of funds or source of wealth, the firm will not
proceed with the transaction and will consider whether a suspicious
transaction report should be filed with the Financial Intelligence Unit.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
8. ONGOING DUE DILIGENCE AND PERIODIC REVIEW
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

CDD does not end at onboarding. The firm will conduct ongoing due
diligence throughout the life of every business relationship.

Transaction monitoring. The firm will monitor transactions carried out
within the business relationship to confirm that they are consistent
with the firm's knowledge of the customer, the customer's business, the
risk profile and the stated source of funds. Transactions that fall
outside the expected pattern will be investigated and documented.

Periodic review. The firm will review and update the CDD file for
every customer at intervals determined by the customer's risk rating.
High-risk customers will be reviewed at least once every twelve months.
Medium-risk customers will be reviewed at least once every twenty-four
months. Low-risk customers will be reviewed at least once every thirty-
six months.

Event-driven review. A review will also be triggered outside the normal
cycle by any of the following events: a material change in the
customer's ownership or control structure, a significant change in the
nature or volume of the customer's transactions, adverse media or
screening alerts relating to the customer or its beneficial owners,
a request from law enforcement or the supervisory authority, or any
other event that causes the firm to question the accuracy of the
information on file.

During each review the firm will confirm that the identification
documents remain valid, that the beneficial ownership information is
still accurate, that the risk rating is still appropriate and that
there is no reason to file a suspicious transaction report.

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
9. CDD FAILURE AND RELATIONSHIP EXIT
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

If the firm is unable to complete the applicable CDD requirements, it
will take the following steps.

It will not open the account, establish the business relationship or
carry out the transaction. If the business relationship has already
been established, it will terminate the relationship in an orderly
manner as soon as reasonably practicable.

The MLRO will assess whether the CDD failure, taken together with any
other information available, gives rise to a suspicion of money
laundering or terrorist financing. If so, the MLRO will file a
suspicious transaction report with the Financial Intelligence Unit
before or at the time of termination.

The firm will document the reasons for the CDD failure and the steps
taken, including any decision to file or not to file a suspicious
transaction report. This documentation will be retained in the
customer file for the full retention period.

The firm will take care not to alert the customer to the fact that a
suspicious transaction report has been or may be filed (tipping-off
prohibition).

-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
10. RECORD-KEEPING
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

All CDD records will be retained for a minimum of ten years from the
date of the transaction or the date on which the business relationship
ended, whichever is later. This retention period is consistent with
the requirement of Federal Decree-Law No. 10 of 2025.

Records include, but are not limited to, copies of identification
documents, verification results, beneficial ownership records, source
of funds and source of wealth documentation, risk assessments, ongoing
monitoring records, periodic review notes, screening results and all
internal correspondence relating to the CDD file.

Records will be stored securely and in a manner that allows them to be
retrieved promptly and provided to the supervisory authority, the
Financial Intelligence Unit or law enforcement upon request.
